Abstract

Bura-Pabir (bwr) is a Chadic language spoken by around 551,000 speakers, primarily identifying as Bura-Pabir, in Northeastern Nigeria (Eberhard et al., 2025). Bura-Pabir has traditionally been analyzed for its morphosyntax (Hartmann & Zimmermann, 2012), but an equally interesting research avenue is available in a cross-linguistic comparison between English and Bura w/r/t how and what conditions projective contents emerge. Through a typological comparison between the two languages using known triggers of projective contents in English and Family of Sentence variants in the spirit of Tonhauser et al. (2013), this investigation intends to demarcate convergences and divergences cross-linguistically in terms of Strong Contextual Felicity Constraints, projection, and Obligatory Local Effect.

Current Status

Based on previously collected FLEx data and a series of elicitation sessions with a native speaker of the Hyil Hawul dialect of Bura, Yakubu Bitrus, we have compiled a descriptive account of how 4 different classes of projective content and their triggers in Bura function across contextually-facilitatory and neutral contexts. These four triggers and their implicatures – the additive adverbial tsúwà, the expressive modifier bzə́bzá (‘damn, spoiled’), the demonstrative determiner ghèndá, and the factive verb sə́ndà – correlate with each Class of projective content proposed in Tonhauser et al. (2013). There is a rough harmony between the behavior we observed in these triggers and their contents for Bura and those attested in both English and Paraguayan Guaraní. We intend to collect more data to further clarify the relationships between and across these languages.
